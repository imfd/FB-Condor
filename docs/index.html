1.Introduction

This report is a supplementary material of the paper“Will we ever understand Facebook's influence on elections? A scientists’ perspective.” We describe our research questions, the Facebook URL Dataset used to perform our experiments, and our results. As described in the main text, our main goal was to study the effect and sharing patterns of rumors and fake news on Facebook in Latin American countries.

The Facebook URL Dataset is a closed-access dataset about shared URLs on the Facebook platform. The dataset includes view, share, and click counts; counts of reactions to posts sharing these URLs (such as likes, loves or angers); demographic data of users sharing the URLs (age range, gender, etc.); and information about the URLs themselves (country where they were shared the most, title, description, etc). In order to protect user privacy, the dataset was postprocessed by applying privacy-protection methods, such as differential privacy, suppression, and generalization of attributes.

We define two categories of URLs as our objects of study:

  • Category 1: URLs pointing to content generated by professional, reputable news media. We also refer to this category as “main” throughout this document.
  • Category 2: URLs pointing to content generated by sources previously known for publishing fake news or debunked rumors. We also refer to this category as “fake” throughout this document.

Our experiments attempted to find differences between these two categories, in particular, during the latest presidential election periods of two latin american countries, Chile and Colombia.

The remainder of this document is structured as follows. Section 2 describes our research questions. Section 3 presents an exploratory analysis of our dataset. Section 4 gives an overview of our methods and experiments. Section 5 presents all our results. Finally, Section 6 summarizes all results in a table and ends with a brief conclusion.

2. Research Questions

Our project aimed to answer the following research questions for two target countries, Chile and Colombia.

RQ1. Are there significant differences between URLs (in terms of number of views and reactions) from professional news media (Category 1) and URLs from sources that are known for publishing fake news (Category 2) in both Chile and Colombia?

RQ2. Which features of a URL present significant differences for these two categories for both Chile and Colombia?

RQ3. Does the election period (pre-election or post-election) in which the URL was created affect these differences for both Chile and Colombia?

3. Dataset description

The Facebook URL Dataset describes data about URLs shared on Facebook, and actions performed on posts sharing those URLs on Facebook. The dataset consists of two tables: URL Attributes and URL Breakdowns. The full codebook of the dataset can be found here.

The URL Attributes table (AT) contains information about the URLs themselves. It has attributes such as the “sanitized” URL (after removing possibly identifying information), the date and time of the first post that shared it on Facebook, the  number of spam, hate speech, and false news flag reports, and the country where it was shared most times in the span of the dataset. For a URL to be included in this dataset, it had to be shared publicly by at least one hundred users (plus Laplacian-distributed noise). The table contains about 38 million URLs from 46 countries, spanning from January 2017 to August, 2019.

The URL Breakdowns table (BT) contains information about actions performed by groups of users to posts containing URLs on Facebook. A group of users is defined by a tuple (year, month, country, age bracket, gender), meaning that a row of this table corresponds to the actions given to a specific URL by a group of users of a certain country, age bracket, gender, during a specific year-month. There are 31 year-months, three genders, and seven age brackets. There also is a political affinity computed column for U.S. users only, with five possible values. This gives about 767 billion rows in total.

The actions described in the URL Breakdowns table are the following: post views, post shares, number of shares without clicking the link, number of comments, number of clicks on the URL, and reactions to the posts, such as “likes,” “loves,” “hahas,” “sorrys,” “angers,” and “wows.”

All numeric attributes, such as the number of shares or hate speech reports, have noise added to satisfy the differential privacy measures imposed by Facebook. This added noise induces measurement error to each of these values. To operationalize this requirement, zero-mean normally distributed noise was added to each value independently (see Table 1).

In our case, we defined groups of URLs based on whether the URL was first published before or after election day in a given country (pre-election, post-election), and whether it came from a domain known to have published misinformative articles in the past (category 2), or from traditional or mainstream media (category 1). 4. Methods and Experiments

To answer our questions, we conducted two types of analyses:

  1. Descriptive analysis: we use descriptive methods, such as descriptive statistics, visualization, and prototype vector representation of the data.
  2. Predictive analysis: we train statistical or machine learning models on the data and assess the predictive capability of these models between different groups of interest.

These analyses were conducted by performing the following experiments:

E1. We calculated summary statistics of all variables for the two groups in both periods. (Descriptive)

E2. We performed data visualization techniques for comparing prototype multi-dimensional vectors of the URLs from these two groups. (Descriptive)

E3. We conducted Mood's median tests for each URL variable using the Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons. The null hypothesis here is that the median of each variable is equal for both groups. (Descriptive)

E4. We trained classification models (i.e., decision trees) using independent training and testing partitions for classifying a URL into a category. (Predictive)

E5. We used the Boruta feature selection technique to identify relevant variables for classifying a URL into a category. (Predictive)

E6.  We used PrivacyUnbiased and Facebook’s svinfer libraries to fit linear regression models using the category as a binary response variable. In this library the estimators are corrected according to the DP-noise of each variable. (Predictive)

5. Experiments and Results

E1. Calculating summary statistics

We can expect the random noise to cancel itself when computing the mean of a sample with sufficient number of observations. Recall that each value of a given numeric attribute in the dataset has been added with an independent zero-mean, normally distributed value (also called classical measurement error).

Our goal here was to compare the averages between attributes and groups and observed that in both countries –Chile and Colombia–, URLs shared post-election had less views and clicks than in the pre-election period.

Since the frequency distribution of our fake and main categories was significantly different (ie., mainstream media have much more URLs), we created a third category, called "Regular domains", as a control group. The idea of this group is to help us determine whether the differences found in our target groups (categories 1 and 2) are not disguised by frequency differences.

We generate a sample of URLs similar to fake and mainstream domains, at least according to the number of URLs per domain in the sample. In order to achieve this, we filtered the Breakdowns table  to consider URLs corresponding to the top 100 domains in terms of number of URLs per domain that were published during the pre and post election periods. More precisely,  the “regular” domain category was sampled from URLs that are not mainstream nor fake, using a matching strategy: for each domain in the fake sample, find the 3 nearest neighbours from the mainstream domains using views, clicks and shares as features.

The procedure to get the corresponding breakdowns is as follows:

Given configuration (country=X, period=Y):

  • Filter rows from AT such that:
  • public_shares_top_country is X
  • date(first_post_time) is within Y
  • The output is a set of tuples (url_rid, parent_domain)
  • Filter rows from BT such that:
  • url_rid is contained in the list obtained from AT above
  • year_month covers the period Y
  • country is X
  • The output is a set of tuples (url_rid, age_bracket, gender, year_month, views, clicks, shares, likes, loves, hahas, wows, sorrys, angers, comments, share_without_clicks)

Where X can be Chile or Colombia and Y can be a pre-election or post-election period.

The main findings of this analysis for the case of Chile for each research question can be summarized as follows:

  • Q1. We observed differences between the two categories in almost all the features, except loves, share_without_clicks, and shares.
  • Q2. The features with highest differences in the two categories were sorrys (7 times more in Category 1 than 2), wows (3.7 times), views (3.1 times), clicks (2.2 times), hahas (1.9 times), and likes (1.8 times).
  • Q3. By comparing features in the same category in different periods, we observed the following differences.
  • Category 1: sorrys (2.1 times more in the post-election period), angers (1.6 times), wows (1.3 times).
  • Category 2: hahas (2.4 times more in the post-election period), share_without_clicks (1.6 times), shares (1.5 times)

And for the case of Colombia:

  • Q1. We observed differences between the two categories in all the features.
  • Q2. The features with highest differences in the two categories were views (7.8 times more in category 1), sorrys (7.1 times), hahas (5.2 times) clicks (4.7 times), and likes (3.9 times)
  • Q3. By comparing features in the same category in different periods, we observed the following differences.
  • Category 1: sorrys (1.5 times more in post-election), angers (1.2 times), and views (1.2 times more in the pre-election period).
  • Category 2: views (1.4 times more in pre-election), clicks (1.3 times more in pre-election), and sorrys (1.2 times in post-election)

E2. Data visualization techniques for comparing prototype multi-dimensional vectors

Another way to understand the difference between the groups of interest is to measure the change in the number of user actions (view, click, like, etc.) w.r.t. a domain from pre-election period to post-election period. For instance, to understand whether a news source known to have published unreliable or misinformative content has received more or less views after election day. In this analysis we considered a URL domain (a news source) as the unit of analysis in order to have a large enough sample to cancel out the noise. We represented a domain as a vector of the mean of selected attributes, in this case views, clicks, and shares, and observed the change in euclidean distance between the vector computed from data pre-election and post-election.

E3. Hypothesis tests to compare medians

We then wanted to observe if those differences were statistically significant. We chose a non-parametric test as we do not know the underlying distribution of the data behind the noise. Additionally, user actions on digital content often form skewed distributions and we believe the median of the distribution could be a better option in order to compare two groups. In this case, we conducted Mood’s median hypothesis test to compare two groups (e.g., median likes of URLs from disreputable sources posted on pre-election period versus post-election period), and applied Bonferroni correction on the p-values in order to avoid Type I errors. We found some statistically significant differences between medians, and commonalities across the two studied countries.

Under two settings (differences between attributes in Category 1 and 2, and differences in different election periods), we found some significant differences between attribute medians. However, the differences are not the same between the two countries. For instance, for views and clicks there are significant differences between their medians, for both countries, but differences in shares and share_without_clicks are significant for Colombia and not for Chile, and vice versa.

However, we also observed that the noise could be potentially skewing the results, depending on the underlying distribution. For example, when performing simulations, we observed that if the underlying data follows a lognormal distribution, plus added noise, the median tests could fail due to Type I errors. This means that the added noise could affect the results depending on the underlying distribution of the data.

E4. Classification models

We conducted a predictive analysis in order to determine if there is a signal in the data that would allow us to distinguish between groups of URLs. For instance, if it is possible to train a classifier with reasonable predictive performance in order to tell apart URLs from disreputable sources and mainstream sources, then it would suggest that there is a strong signal in the data despite the noise. We trained decision trees using the rpart R library with default values for all combinations of binary problems for our three categories and two countries using standard training/testing splits. We found that the views attribute is the most useful to distinguish URLs from disreputable and mainstream domains. This is not surprising given the “by definition” popularity aspect of mainstream domains.

E6.  PrivacyUnbiased Linear regression

Finally, we performed linear regression experiments using an adapted method to overcome the known measurement error given by the amount of noise added to the data. We used the R PrivacyUnbiased library and the python Statistical Valid Inference (svinfer) python library provided by Facebook. Note that the latter is a python adaptation of the former.   The purpose is to compare the linear coefficients determined to be statistically significant with this method against the variables reported as significant/relevant according to previous experiments (e.g., Mood's median test, Boruta).

A major shortcoming of these libraries is a potential mismatch between the nature of our problem and the method employed: our problem is a classification task with a binary dependent variable, both PrivacyUnbiased and svinfer are designed to work with continuous response variables. We decided to treat svinfer and PrivacyUnbiased regression models as  linear probability models, which is the standard interpretation of applying linear regression models to binary classification tasks (a logistic regression is a more appropriate choice, but a DP variant of this model has not been developed as far as we know).

Overall, our results indicated that a) some variables are significantly different for the two groups, b) some variables are important for distinguishing between these two groups, and c) the decision tree model exhibits important prediction capabilities for some tasks (F1 scores above 0.5 for both countries). However, we could not find a clear correspondence with the attributes identified as relevant in the different experiments. In fact, the results obtained in experiments 3 and 6 showed major inconsistencies.

If we compare the classification performance between the PrivacyUnbiased linear model and the decision tree, the latter was significantly better than the former. While the PrivacyUnbiased linear model considers almost all attributes as statistically significant, a more standard statistical test such as Moods’ median test tends to consider only a fraction of the attributes as significant (e.g., reactions such as sorry or anger). It appears to us that factors such as the small sample size plus the level of noise, and the lack of mature tools to handle data of this nature, made it impossible to reach clear conclusions or findings. Consequently, we could not produce reliable answers to our research questions.
